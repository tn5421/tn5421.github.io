Lear 16 Aug 2008 20:46

I don't think that it's that the RNG is broken. In fact, I don't really see how the RNG could be broken, unless of course, he's purposely written code to make it return the previous result more often than others.

What I think is happening, is that your fooling yourself into thinking that it's broken. You're finding unlikely results (or results that appear unlikely) and isolating them, rather than weighting them appropriately by their likelyhood.

For example, let's say there are 4 possible outcomes for a randomly determined event, all equally likely. We'll call them A, B, C, and D.

Now, which is more likely, getting two of the same in a row, or getting all 4 in a row (any order) without any duplicates?

Getting two As in a row, and by a fairly significant margin.
Two in a row = 1/4 chance
All 4, no duplicates = 6/64 chance

Now, which would you notice more, two of the same in a row or getting all 4 without duplicates?

Getting two of the same kind in a row, and you'd think of it as being unusual. That's not really fair, though. You're ignoring all of the other equally unlikely or even more unlikely results.

Now, which is more likely, getting A,A,A,A or getting A, B, D, A?

They're equally unlikely. Both have a 1/256 chance of occuring. But would you really stop to think, "Wow, ABDA, that's surprising. I can't believe that happened."? Of course not, and you shouldn't think that. You shouldn't think that about AAAA either, but it's inevitable that you're going to.

The same works on a scale similar to the game's. Let's say that you're trying to level Annie. Her level mission, Teach a Class, is a Nin B-rank, so you just run Nin missions to increase her chances of leveling.

Now, the first mission, you end up getting Defend the WhiteEye Princess, and you think, "Damn! I should have brought Stalkergirl too!" Or maybe you've already leveled her and think, "Damn! Took forever to get her mission when I was trying to level her, and now I get it when I no longer want it!" Well, so you complete (or smokebomb the mission) and the next mission you get is Defend the WhiteEye Princess again. By now you're probably thinking that the RNG is hating you, and you might be right, but it's just random, not due to any bias it has. The odds are 1/11 that you'd get that mission again, no less likely than getting say, Row a Gondola, or Teach a Class, both of which you'd have probably been happy with.

Now, let's say you've run 20 missions, and that mission you need still hasn't shown up. You think that it's been mean to you, but what are the actual odds of it not coming up in 20 missions? About 15%, not horribly unlikely.

The prize wheel could be worse, and it's really not possible to assess because we don't know the exact odds of each prize. Now, have I gotten two Super-rares in a row before? Yes. Has that happened to me after that? No. Have I had plenty of days where the most valuable thing I won was a shuriken? Yup. It goes both ways, and everything in between.

Ren Po Ken 17 Aug 2008 16:50

There are hot streaks, cold streaks, and streaks of hot pictures of Terri. In any random system, streaks are inevitable. Also inevitable, streaks end. If you get a monkey to pound away on a keyboard long enough, you will eventually get the complete works of Konami. Long before that happens, you will get various Stephen King novels, The Davinci Code, reality TV shows in .avi format, the source code for the BvS SWATBU content, and a pissed off monkey. These are all steaks. A streak is simply the human mind (I apologize if you are not human) ascribing meaning to a slice of random values.

I highly doubt, given the fact that there is an RNG built into every programming language, that McMasters went out and made his own. I find it even more unlikely that if he did, that it would be any less random than the ones that come with a language. The RNG is no more broken than any other RNG out there.
